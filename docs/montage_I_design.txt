Cinelingus Montage Design Decisions (Design Authority)
Design Philosophy

Cinelingus is not a video splicer.

It is a cinematic composer.

Every output should feel as though an unseen editor intentionally assembled existing films into a new work. The audience should notice strange narrative relationships, not software artifacts.

The editor's first obligation is therefore cinematic integrity. Every later transformation (dialogue replacement, chronology, infection, translation, identity exchange) operates within that constraint unless a filter explicitly declares otherwise.

1. The Unit of Composition

Decision

The primary selectable object is the cinematic moment, not the individual shot.

A cinematic moment consists of one or more consecutive shots that together express one coherent visual idea.

Examples:

a character noticing something
entering a room
sitting down
one complete exchange
one uninterrupted action
one establishing view
one reaction sequence

The engine may internally operate on shots, but filter authors should think exclusively in moments.

Moments may contain:

one shot
many shots
no dialogue
multiple speakers

They should almost never begin or end arbitrarily.

2. Clean Segment Definition

A clean segment begins and ends where an experienced film editor would naturally cut.

Default requirements:

✓ bounded by detected cuts

✓ complete gestures

✓ complete gaze shifts

✓ completed camera movement

✓ completed spoken thought

✓ no interrupted mouth movement

✓ no interrupted physical action

Optional handles before and after every segment.

Exceptions
Long takes

Treat internally as virtual sub-scenes.

Never force an arbitrary cut merely because no edit exists.

Instead locate natural pauses:

completed actions
pauses in speech
stillness
framing changes
Dissolves

Treat dissolve duration as indivisible.

Never begin or end inside a dissolve.

Fades

May begin immediately after fade-in.

May end immediately before fade-out.

Never inside the fade.

Establishing shots

Highly desirable.

Excellent transition material.

May appear without dialogue.

Reaction shots

Among the most reusable assets.

High priority.

Partial spoken sentence

Avoid whenever possible.

If unavoidable:

allow only sentence continuation—

never sentence interruption.

3. Audio Philosophy

This is where I differ most from the proposal.

The visual edit and the sound edit should become partially independent.

Every filter declares an Audio Law.

Supported laws:

Original Reality

Picture and synchronized production sound remain together.

Only montage changes.

Useful for Flashback.

Dialogue Replacement

Original ambience preserved.

Dialogue replaced.

Current Cinelingus default.

Cinematic Bed

Continuous ambience and music flow across cuts.

Dialogue changes normally.

This often makes rapid montage feel surprisingly professional.

Audio Leads Picture

Dialogue begins before visual cut.

Classic cinema grammar.

Highly recommended.

Picture Leads Audio

Visual changes before speech.

Useful for suspense.

Constructed Reality

Entire soundtrack generated from:

dialogue

ambience

music

effects

independently.

Ultimate long-term goal.

Default:

Dialogue Replacement + Continuous Ambient Bed.

That produces the least jarring montage.

4. Montage Grammar

The montage renderer should obey one governing relationship.

Never several equally.

Priority order:

Filter Law

Speech intelligibility

Visual continuity

Rhythm

Source diversity

Novelty

Not vice versa.

Supported montage relationships include:

Identity

Memory

Cause

Contrast

Escalation

Symmetry

Repetition

Irony

Association

Collision

Silence

The filter chooses one.

Everything else supports it.

5. Cross-Film Balance

I like the proposed 60%.

I would strengthen it.

Every required source must become visually recognizable.

Suggested defaults:

Anchor film:

40–60%

Secondary:

20–40%

Tertiary:

10–25%

No film should disappear for long enough that the audience forgets it exists.

6. Speech Integrity

This should be stricter.

Dialogue is sacred.

Rules:

Never cut inside:

word

syllable

mouth opening

breath onset

Never truncate:

question

answer

thought

joke

reaction

Dialogue may continue across cuts.

Cinema has done this for nearly a century.

Reaction shots over continuing dialogue should become one of Cinelingus's defining characteristics.

Off-screen speech:

strongly encouraged.

Subtitles should never influence edit decisions.

They are presentation.

Not cinema.

7. Movement

Movement continuity matters less than movement completion.

Bad:

person starts standing

CUT

already walking

Good:

person finishes standing

CUT

walking elsewhere

Excellent:

matching motion

same screen direction

similar speed

similar framing

This should become a bonus rather than a requirement.

8. Target Shape

I think shorter is better.

Current recommendation:

Standard Output

90–150 seconds

Quick Preview

30–60 seconds

Extended

3–6 minutes

Experimental

Unlimited

Typical segment:

4–12 seconds

Average:

7 seconds

Minimum distinct moments:

8

Preferred:

12–25

Every montage should contain:

Beginning

Development

Climax

Resolution

Even absurd films deserve an ending.

They should never simply stop because the timer expired.

9. Filter Authorship

I largely agree.

One addition:

Every filter should declare four independent laws.

Visual Law

What imagery may be selected.

Temporal Law

How chronology behaves.

Dialogue Law

How speech changes.

Audio Law

How sound behaves.

That makes filters composable.

For example:

Flashback

Visual:
earlier scenes

Temporal:
reverse

Dialogue:
unchanged

Audio:
original

Dream

Visual:
free association

Temporal:
collapsed

Dialogue:
translated

Audio:
continuous ambience

Possession

Visual:
speaker tracking

Temporal:
linear

Dialogue:
identity exchange

Audio:
replacement

No special-case renderer is required.

Only different laws.

10. Positive References

Instead of film titles, I'd specify editing principles that are recognizable across cinema:

Excellent entrance

Enter immediately after a completed cut, with the subject already established in frame.

Excellent exit

Leave after a completed action, before visual energy dissipates.

Excellent reaction cut

Dialogue continues while another character silently processes what was said.

Excellent montage density

Frequent cuts that each contribute new information, without becoming visually exhausting.

Poor interruption

A gesture begins in one segment and completes in another without intentional continuity.

Poor source dominance

One film occupies so much screen time that the others become forgettable.

One Additional Principle

I would add one concept that I think becomes foundational to Cinelingus:

Respect before Surprise.

Every cut should first succeed as a competent piece of film editing. Only then should it surprise the audience.
